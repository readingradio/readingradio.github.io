Part 1
What is a Metagenome?
Comparing genes across entire communities of organisms

The Three Core Terms

Metagenome
A collective of genomes from a community of organisms in an environmental sample. Communities can be holobionts (host + microbes), lakes, soils, etc.
Microbiome
The community of microorganisms that associate and interact with a host — your gut microbiome, skin microbiome, etc.
Holobiont / Metaorganism
The combination of host + its microbiome. Selection can act on the holobiont as a unit, not just on the host alone.
Why metagenomics?
Most microbes cannot be cultured in a lab. Metagenomics lets us study these nonculturable organisms directly from environmental samples.

Good Target Genes for Metagenomics

Not every gene works well as a "barcode" for community profiling. Ideal genes are:

  • Similar across taxa — present in all organisms of interest (so you can compare)
  • Relatively short — practical for sequencing
  • Have a variable region flanked by conserved regions — conserved ends = universal primers; variable center = tells species apart
  • Occur only once per genome — avoids ambiguity
  • Unlikely to be horizontally transferred — ensures the gene tracks organismal evolution
Primary examples: 16S rRNA (bacteria/archaea), ITS (fungi), 18S rRNA (eukaryotes). These genes have conserved flanking regions for PCR primers and variable internal regions for species identification.

The 16S rRNA gene has 9 variable regions (V1–V9). Researchers choose which variable region(s) to target based on the level of resolution needed and the taxa of interest.

Why the 97% Threshold?

When comparing 16S sequences, organisms with ≥97% sequence identity are grouped into the same OTU (roughly genus level). This threshold:

Was formalized in the 1980s–90s and led to a dramatic increase in the number of microbial species described — from a few hundred to thousands per year. The OTU concept is essential for cataloguing novel microbial diversity without culturing.
Part 2
Collection & Sequencing Pipeline
From environmental sample to assembled genome sequences

The Metagenomic Workflow

1. Sample collection — gut tissue, soil, lake water, or any environmental material containing the community
↓
2. DNA extraction — extract DNA from all organisms in the sample simultaneously
↓
3. PCR amplification — use universal primers targeting conserved flanking regions of 16S (or ITS/18S) to amplify the variable region across all taxa
↓
4. DNA library preparation — attach sequencing adapters to the amplified fragments
↓
5. Next Generation Sequencing (NGS / Illumina) — millions of reads generated in parallel; highly sensitive and fast
↓
6. Sequence analysis & assembly — reads are clustered into OTUs, overlapping reads assembled into contigs
↓
7. Database matching & annotation — sequences matched against NCBI, GOLD, UCSC databases to identify organisms and functions

Key Genomic Databases

DatabasePurpose
NCBI / GenBankLargest repository of nucleotide sequences, literature (PubMed), genes, proteins, clinical data
GOLD (JGI)Genomes Online Database — metadata for genome and metagenome projects worldwide
UCSC Genome BrowserVisualization and annotation of assembled genomes; good for exploring specific loci

Pros and Cons of Metagenomic Studies

✓ Advantages
  • Can capture nonculturable organisms — most microbes can't be grown in a lab
  • High sensitivity — detects low-abundance taxa
  • Fast turnaround with NGS platforms
✗ Limitations
  • High cost — sequencing and analysis
  • Requires bioinformatic expertise
  • Contamination and low-abundance taxa can interfere
Key takeaway: Multiple approaches in combination are the most effective. Metagenomic sequencing works best alongside culturing, microscopy, and functional assays.
Part 3
OTUs, Diversity Metrics & Analysis
How we quantify and compare community composition
OTUs & Diversity Analysis — Slide Deck

Operational Taxonomic Units (OTUs)

OTUs are groupings of organisms based on genetic similarity — essentially a computational proxy for "species" in microbial ecology. Also called phylotypes.

Standard threshold: sequences sharing ≥97% 16S rRNA identity are placed in the same OTU (roughly genus level). Below 97% → different OTUs.

OTUs are especially useful for novel microbial species that have never been cultured or formally described. You can track and compare them without knowing what they are.

α Diversity vs. β Diversity

MetricWhat it measuresVisualized by
α (Alpha) DiversityDiversity within a single group/site — how many different species are there, and how evenly distributed?Shannon index box plots; rarefaction curves
β (Beta) DiversityDiversity between groups — how different are two communities from each other?PCA / PCoA scatter plots; distance matrices
Real example: Mangrove microbiomes have much higher α diversity (Shannon index ~11) than hot springs (~6). β diversity analysis shows these environments cluster into completely separate groups on a PCoA plot.

Principal Component Analysis (PCA)

PCA is a statistical method that groups samples by the similarity of their microbial community sequences. Similar communities cluster together on the plot; dissimilar communities are far apart.

Human microbiome PCA example:
  • Different body sites (oral, gastrointestinal, skin, urogenital) cluster into distinct groups
  • Within the gastrointestinal site alone, samples shift from infant → child → adult → elder along PC1

Each dot = one sample. Distance between dots = difference in community composition. The axes (PC1, PC2) capture the most variance in the data.

Rarefaction Curves

Rarefaction curves plot cumulative OTUs (y-axis) vs. number of sequences sampled (x-axis). They answer: "Are we sampling enough to capture the full diversity?"

Interpretation:
  • Curve that plateaus (flattens) = good sampling; most diversity has been captured (low species richness OR sufficient depth)
  • Curve that keeps rising steeply = insufficient sampling; more sequencing needed; high species richness
Panda example: Giant pandas (bamboo specialists) have much lower gut microbiome diversity than Thompson's gazelles (generalist grazers). The panda curve plateaus early at ~15 OTUs; the gazelle curve rises steeply past ~70 OTUs. Dietary specialization likely reduces microbiome diversity.

Part 4
Holobionts, Coevolution & Selection
When the microbiome shapes fitness — and how hosts and microbes coevolve
Holobiont Selection & Coevolution — Slide Deck

The Microbiome Can Modulate Phenotypic Expression

The microbiome isn't just a passenger — it actively influences the host's phenotype. This means selection can act on the holobiont as a whole unit, not just the host genome.

Obese mouse example: Obese (ob/ob) mice — with the same diet as wild-type mice — have a higher proportion of Firmicutes and a lower proportion of Bacteroidetes in their gut. The microbiome composition correlates with the obese phenotype even when diet is controlled.

This suggests that microbiome composition can contribute to phenotypic differences beyond what the host genome alone predicts.

Attine Ants: A Multi-Species Coevolution System

Leaf-cutter ants (tribe Attini) maintain a complex multi-organism system involving four players:

  1. Attine ant (host) — farms a fungal garden for food
  2. Cultivated fungus (Trachymyrmex spp.) — the ant's food source
  3. Parasitic fungus (Escovopsis) — invades and devastates fungal gardens
  4. Actinobacteria — live on the ant's cuticle and produce antibiotics that inhibit Escovopsis
The three phylogenies (ant, cultivated fungus, Escovopsis) show similar branching patterns — evidence of long-term coevolution — even though Escovopsis is transmitted horizontally.

The Red Queen Hypothesis in Metagenomics Context

Hosts and parasites are always evolving to get around each other's defenses — neither side ever "wins" permanently.

Key experimental evidence: Non-deleterious mutations in a parasite of an evolving host accumulate at a much faster rate than in a parasite of a constant/static host. The parasite must keep mutating just to keep infecting the evolving host — exactly the "running to stay in place" dynamic.

This is directly observable in metagenome data: by tracking mutation rates in parasitic microbes across evolving vs. non-evolving host populations, we can test the Red Queen hypothesis empirically.

Ancient Metagenomics

Metagenomics can be applied to ancient DNA (aDNA) extracted from archaeological materials — teeth, bones, calcified nodules — to reconstruct past microbiomes and diseases.

Example 1 — Medieval Italy: A 14th-century skeleton from Geridu, Italy had calcified nodules containing bacterial fossils. Metagenomic sequencing identified Brucella melitensis as the likely cause of death — confirming a medieval strain closely related to but distinct from contemporary strains.
Example 2 — Neanderthal dental calculus: Ancient DNA from calcified plaque revealed Neanderthal diet (plants, mushrooms) and pathogen exposure — even differentiating Neanderthal vs. modern human microbiome compositions by major bacterial phyla.

Transcriptomics: What Genes Are Being Expressed?

While metagenomics tells us which organisms and genes are present, transcriptomics tells us which genes are actively being transcribed at a given moment.

Method: Target mRNA (not DNA) using specific primers → reverse transcribe to cDNA → sequence.

Marine bacteria example: Photosynthetic marine bacteria (Prochlorococcus) show oscillating gene expression throughout the day — peaking at different times for different metabolic categories (ribosomes, carbon fixation, photosynthesis). Non-photosynthetic bacteria (Pelagibacter) living alongside them show similar oscillation patterns — their transcription is coupled to the photosynthetic bacteria's nutrient output.

Metabolomics: Composition vs. Function

A key insight from metabolomics: the specific microbial taxa can vary enormously between individuals and environments, but metabolic pathways remain relatively stable.

Key finding: Three environmental microbiomes (whale falls, Sargasso Sea, agricultural soil) have proportions of key metabolic genes (carbohydrate metabolism, energy metabolism, amino acid metabolism) similar to human and mouse gut microbiomes — despite completely different community compositions. Similar functions can emerge from radically different organisms.

Within the human microbiome: microbial taxa vary greatly between individuals for the same body site, but metabolic pathway representation is much more uniform within a healthy population — suggesting strong functional selection on the holobiont.

Functional Metagenomics: Mining the Resistome

Functional metagenomics doesn't just characterize who's there — it finds specific functional genes by selecting for phenotypes.

Resistome pipeline (antibiotic resistance genes):

1. Extract genomic DNA from fecal sample
↓
2. Fragment DNA into ~2 kb pieces and ligate into plasmid vectors
↓
3. Transfect into indicator host strain (e.g., E. coli)
↓
4. Grow on antibiotic-containing medium → only resistant clones survive
↓
5. PCR amplify → sequence → annotate the resistance gene
This approach harvests the entire "resistome" — all antibiotic resistance genes present in a microbiome — without needing to know what organisms they came from.

Review Questions

Q1
Define metagenome, microbiome, and holobiont. How do they relate to each other?
A metagenome is the collective of all genomes from a community of organisms in an environmental sample (gut, lake, soil, etc.). A microbiome is the specific community of microorganisms associated with a particular host (e.g., the human gut microbiome). A holobiont (or metaorganism) is the combination of a host and its microbiome treated as a single biological unit. The relationship: the holobiont's microbiome contributes to its metagenome; studying the metagenome gives us data on the microbiome's genetic content and function.
Q2
What properties make 16S rRNA an ideal target gene for metagenomic community profiling? Why might you use ITS instead?
16S rRNA is ideal for bacteria/archaea because it is: (1) present in all prokaryotes (similar across taxa), (2) relatively short and practical to sequence, (3) contains a variable region flanked by conserved regions — the conserved regions allow universal PCR primers while the variable region tells species apart, (4) single-copy (occurs once per genome), and (5) unlikely to be horizontally transferred, ensuring it tracks true evolutionary relationships.

ITS (Internal Transcribed Spacer) is used instead when studying fungi, because ITS is the standard fungal barcode gene with similar properties for that kingdom. 18S rRNA is used for eukaryotes more broadly.
Q3
What is an OTU and why is the 97% identity threshold significant? What taxonomic level does it roughly correspond to?
An OTU (Operational Taxonomic Unit) is a group of organisms with ≥97% 16S rRNA sequence similarity — a computational proxy for species in microbial ecology. It is especially useful for novel, nonculturable microbes that have never been formally described. The 97% threshold roughly corresponds to genus level taxonomic grouping. The introduction of this threshold in the 1980s–90s drove a massive increase in microbial species discovery.
Q4
Distinguish α diversity from β diversity. Give one method for visualizing each.
α diversity = diversity within a single community/site — measures species richness and evenness at one location. Visualized by: Shannon index box plots, rarefaction curves (OTUs vs. sequences).

β diversity = diversity between communities — measures how different two communities are from each other. Visualized by: PCA/PCoA scatter plots (samples that cluster together have similar community composition), Bray-Curtis dissimilarity matrices.
Q5
How do you interpret a rarefaction curve? What does a curve that has plateaued tell you vs. one that is still rising steeply?
A rarefaction curve plots cumulative OTUs discovered vs. number of sequences sampled.

A curve that plateaus/flattens means that additional sequencing is unlikely to reveal new OTUs — sampling depth is sufficient and most of the community's diversity has been captured. This community has relatively low species richness OR has been thoroughly sampled.

A curve that continues rising steeply means there are still many undiscovered OTUs — the community has high species richness or the sample is undersequenced. The giant panda gut microbiome plateaus early (~15 OTUs), reflecting low diversity due to bamboo specialization. The Thompson's gazelle microbiome keeps rising past 70+ OTUs, reflecting high diversity from a more varied diet.
Q6
Describe the attine ant holobiont system. What are the four key players, and what evidence is there for long-term coevolution?
The attine ant system involves four players: (1) the attine ant (host), (2) the cultivated fungus (Trachymyrmex species) farmed by the ant for food, (3) the parasitic fungus (Escovopsis) that invades and destroys fungal gardens, and (4) Actinobacteria living on the ant's cuticle that produce antibiotics inhibiting Escovopsis.

Evidence for coevolution: The phylogenetic trees of ants, their cultivated fungi, and Escovopsis all show similar branching patterns, indicating long-term co-diversification. This is remarkable because Escovopsis is horizontally transmitted (not vertically inherited) — yet it has tracked ant and fungal evolution closely, implying tight coevolutionary pressure.
Q7
What is the key finding from comparing microbial taxa composition vs. metabolic pathway representation across different body sites and individuals?
The key finding is: microbial community composition (which taxa are present) varies enormously between individuals and body sites, but metabolic pathway representation is much more stable and uniform.

Within one body site, different individuals have very different microbial species — yet the same metabolic pathways (carbohydrate metabolism, ATP synthesis, amino acid transport) are present at similar proportions. This also holds across environments: whale fall, soil, and ocean microbiomes have metabolic gene profiles similar to human gut microbiomes despite completely different taxa. This suggests that functional selection acts on the holobiont — what matters is that essential metabolic functions are maintained, not which specific organisms perform them.
Q8
What is functional metagenomics? Describe how you would use it to identify antibiotic resistance genes (the "resistome") in a gut microbiome.
Functional metagenomics screens for specific functional genes by selecting for phenotypes, not just sequencing. To find antibiotic resistance genes: (1) extract genomic DNA from a fecal sample, (2) fragment into ~2 kb pieces, (3) ligate fragments into plasmid vectors, (4) transfect into E. coli indicator cells, (5) grow on antibiotic-containing medium — only cells containing resistance genes survive, (6) PCR amplify those genes from surviving colonies, (7) sequence and annotate. This harvests the entire "resistome" — all resistance genes present — regardless of which organism they originally came from.
Q9
How does the Red Queen hypothesis connect to metagenomic data on host-parasite coevolution? What evidence supports it?
The Red Queen hypothesis predicts that hosts and parasites are always co-evolving — each must keep adapting just to maintain fitness against the other. Metagenomic evidence: when parasites infect an evolving host (one that is changing genetically), the parasite accumulates non-deleterious mutations at a faster rate than a parasite infecting a genetically constant host. This shows that parasite evolution is driven by and tracks host evolution — supporting the coevolutionary arms race model. The attine ant system also supports this: even though Escovopsis is horizontally transmitted, its phylogeny mirrors the ant and fungal phylogenies, showing it has tracked holobiont evolution over millions of years.
Q10
Name at least three pros and three cons of metagenomic approaches. What is the general recommendation for maximizing research effectiveness?
Pros: (1) Can detect nonculturable organisms — the majority of microbes cannot be grown in a lab; (2) High sensitivity — detects even low-abundance taxa; (3) Fast turnaround with modern NGS platforms like Illumina.

Cons: (1) High cost — sequencing and computational analysis; (2) Requires significant bioinformatics expertise; (3) Contamination and low-abundance taxa can interfere with results.

Recommendation: Multiple approaches in combination are the most effective — metagenomics is best used alongside culturing, microscopy, transcriptomics, metabolomics, and functional assays to get a complete picture of community composition and function.
