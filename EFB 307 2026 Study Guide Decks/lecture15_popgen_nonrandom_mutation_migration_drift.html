Big Picture: HWE Violations Revisited

This lecture focuses on four of the five HWE assumptions and what happens when they are broken. The key distinction remains:

Genotype frequencies change only
Assortative mating → Ho ≠ He, but allele frequencies stay constant
Allele frequencies change (Δq ≠ 0)
Mutation, migration, drift, and selection can all shift allele frequencies over generations
Section 5
Changes in Genotype Frequencies

Heterozygosity: Expected vs. Observed

He (Expected)
= 2pq. What proportion of individuals should be heterozygous under HWE given the allele frequencies
Ho (Observed)
= (# Aa + aA) / total individuals. The actual proportion of heterozygotes counted in the population
At HWE equilibrium: Ho = He
Deviations (Ho ≠ He) imply non-random (assortative) mating — but allele frequencies are unchanged

Assortative Mating: Two Directions

Positive Assortative Mating (Inbreeding)
"Like prefers like" — similar phenotypes mate preferentially

→ Homozygotes increase
→ Heterozygotes decrease
→ Ho < He
Negative Assortative Mating (Outbreeding)
"Opposites attract" — different phenotypes mate preferentially

→ Heterozygotes increase
→ Homozygotes decrease
→ Ho > He
Key reminder: In both cases, allele frequencies (p and q) do not change. Only the distribution across genotype classes is affected.

Worked Example: Sickle-Cell Anemia

Sickle-cell anemia is recessive. Frequency of the disease (zz) = 1 in 10,000. What is the frequency of asymptomatic carriers?

1
Find q from disease frequency
q² = 0.0001 → q = √0.0001 = 0.01
2
Find p
p = 1 − q = 1 − 0.01 = 0.99
3
Calculate expected heterozygosity (carrier frequency)
He = 2pq = 2 × 0.01 × 0.99 = 0.0198 ≈ 1.98%
About 1 in 50 people are asymptomatic carriers — far more than the 1 in 10,000 who actually express the disease.

Calculating p from Observed Genotype Frequencies

If you know how many individuals have each genotype, you can work backwards:

p = freq(AA) + ½ × freq(Aa/aA)
q = 1 − p
AA individuals contribute 100% A gametes; heterozygotes contribute 50% A gametes
Example: White-throated sparrow morphs
60% gray-white (Ww), 10% white striped (WW), 30% gray striped (ww)
p = 0.10 + ½(0.60) = 0.40 | q = 0.60
He = 2(0.40)(0.60) = 0.48 | Ho = 0.60 → Ho > He → outbreeding

Inbreeding in Detail

Extreme positive assortative mating → inbreeding. This splits a population into homozygous sub-populations (AA and aa).

  • A mutation arises on one chromosome in one individual and creates a new haplotype
  • This mutation + surrounding haplotype are passed through generations
  • A descendant who mates with another carrier of that same common ancestor will be homozygous by descent — identical haplotypes on both chromosomes
Real-world examples: domestic dogs (selective breeding), royal families (consanguinity), isolated human populations
Section 6
Changes in Allele Frequencies: Mutation

Mutation = Introduction of a New Allele

Mutation is the ultimate source of all genetic variation. It introduces a brand-new allele into the gene pool — changing the total allele count and thus allele frequencies.

Important: The mutation rate alone is usually too low to meaningfully shift allele frequencies in a large population on its own. Other forces must act together with it.

Two Fates of a New Mutation

Purifying Selection
The mutation is deleterious — reduces fitness. Natural selection filters it out. New allele stays rare or disappears.

Examples: monstrose (abnormal growth) mutations in conifers and cacti; fasciation in pea plants (fa frameshift)
Positive Selection
The mutation confers a fitness advantage. It spreads through the population and may reach fixation.

Example: small-stomata allele in arid-adapted Populus trichocarpa populations

Factors That Amplify Mutation's Effect on Allele Frequencies

  • High mutation rate → more new alleles per generation → larger effect on pool
  • Small population size → each new allele represents a larger fraction of the gene pool; drift can fix it
  • Non-random mating → inbreeding can rapidly increase frequency of a new allele through homozygosity
  • Limited migration → the allele can't be diluted by incoming alleles from other populations (bottleneck effect)
  • Positive selection / differential fertility → individuals with the allele reproduce more → allele spreads
Section 7
Changes in Allele Frequencies: Migration

Gene Flow vs. Isolation

Migration (gene flow) is the movement of alleles between populations. It has opposite effects depending on whether it is allowed or blocked.

Migration Allowed → Homogenization
Alleles from one population enter another. Over time, allele frequencies between the populations converge — they become more similar.
Migration Blocked → Divergence
Each isolated sub-population evolves independently. Allele frequencies drift apart, and each population becomes genetically distinct.

Worked Example: Migration Changes Frequencies

Population 1 (200 individuals): p = 0.70, q = 0.30. Then 20 immigrants arrive with p = 0.60, q = 0.40.

1
Count A alleles after migration
Original: 140 A alleles (from 200 ind.) | Migrants: 24 A alleles (from 20 ind.)
Total A = 140 + 24 = 164
2
Count total alleles
(200 + 20) × 2 = 440 total alleles
3
New allele frequencies
p = (140+24)/240 ≈ 0.68 | q ≈ 0.32 (shifted from original 0.70/0.30)

Real-World Example: Grand Canyon Squirrels

The Grand Canyon acts as a geographic barrier separating two subspecies of the same squirrel, Sciurus aberti:

Abert's Squirrel (South Rim)
S. aberti aberti — light coloration, gray body. Has not been isolated as long; some gene flow possible historically.
Kaibab Squirrel (North Rim)
S. aberti kaibabensis — dark body, white tail. Completely isolated on the Kaibab Plateau. Has diverged substantially.
The Grand Canyon = isolation event → allele frequencies diverge → two distinct sub-populations. A classic example of isolation preventing gene flow and driving differentiation.

Summary: Factors Affecting Migration's Influence

  • Intermixing → introduction of new alleles + homogenization between populations
  • Isolation by migration (active separation) → differentiation over time
  • Isolation by lack of migration (geographic/cultural barriers) → differentiation over time
Section 8
Changes in Allele Frequencies: Genetic Drift

Genetic Drift & Neutral Theory

Every population is a sample of the previous generation. In small samples, random chance causes allele frequencies to fluctuate — this is genetic drift.

  • Drift is stochastic (random) — not driven by fitness advantage
  • Even neutral alleles (no effect on fitness) fluctuate in frequency over generations
  • In small, isolated populations, alleles can become fixed (freq = 1.0) or go extinct (freq = 0) purely by chance
Drift in simulation: starting at p = 0.5 in populations of 100, some populations showed fixation of one allele within ~30 generations — purely by chance.

Population Bottleneck

A bottleneck occurs when a large population is dramatically reduced in size due to catastrophe (disease, disaster, climate), and then re-expands. The surviving individuals are a random, small subset of the original gene pool.

Effect: The new, expanded population has allele frequencies that reflect the random survivors — not the original population. Rare alleles may become common; common alleles may disappear.

Real-World Bottleneck Examples

  • Iceland — 3 separate bottleneck events (Black Death, smallpox, volcanic eruption). Today, Icelandic people carry alleles at frequencies very different from other European populations.
  • Europe (Black Death) — killed >40% of European population in the 14th century. Altered allele frequencies across the continent, especially immune-related genes.

Founder Effect

A special case of bottleneck: a small founding group colonizes a new territory and becomes isolated. The founder group's allele frequencies — however non-representative — define the new population's gene pool.

Iceland (Vikings)
Founded by ~2,000 Vikings. Population grew to ~270,000 with minimal gene flow. Now genetically distinct from mainland Europeans.
French Canadians (Quebec)
Isolated from British colonizers by language and religion. Genetically distinct from other North American populations. Higher frequency of Tay-Sachs (7.5 kb HexA deletion).
Ashkenazi Jews
Religious isolation from surrounding populations over centuries. Higher frequency of Tay-Sachs (4 bp HexA insertion), Gaucher disease, and others.
Tay-Sachs comparison: French Canadians carry a different mutation in HexA (7.5 kb deletion) than Ashkenazi Jews (4 bp insertion) — both cause the same disease but arose independently in two separately isolated founder populations. Only one documented case of a French Canadian carrying the Ashkenazi version.

Effective Population Size (Ne)

Ne is the size of an ideal population that would experience the same amount of genetic drift as the actual population. It accounts for unequal sex ratios and variable reproductive success.

  • Ne is almost always smaller than the census population size
  • Bottlenecks dramatically reduce Ne — even temporarily small populations leave lasting genetic signatures
  • 50/500 rule: Ne > 50 to avoid short-term severe inbreeding; Ne > 500 to minimize drift over the long term — key benchmarks in conservation genetics

Factors Determining Drift's Impact

Increases Drift

  • Small Ne
  • Low migration / isolation
  • Bottleneck or founder event

Reduces Drift

  • Large Ne
  • High gene flow / migration
  • Stable, large population over time

Interaction of Forces

These forces do not act in isolation — they interact to shape allele frequencies:

  • Gene flow is determined jointly by migration rate, population size, and mating pattern — more migration + larger pop + random mating = more gene flow
  • Restricted gene flow (from any combination of isolation, small Ne, or non-random mating) leads to neutral genetic differentiation via drift
  • A mutation at low frequency might normally be lost — but a bottleneck or non-random mating can dramatically amplify its frequency before selection can act
Rule of thumb: Small Ne + isolation = drift dominates. Large Ne + gene flow = selection dominates. Most real populations exist on a spectrum between these extremes.

When close relatives mate, they are more likely to carry the same recessive alleles inherited from a common ancestor (identical by descent). As homozygosity increases, the probability that offspring receive two copies of the same recessive allele rises — making it more likely that a harmful recessive phenotype is expressed. §6 — Mutation

Answer
Mutation rates are typically very low — on the order of 10⁻⁶ to 10⁻⁸ per base pair per generation. In a large population, a single new mutation represents an infinitesimally small fraction of the total gene pool. Without additional forces (selection, drift, non-random mating, or limited migration) to amplify or spread it, the mutation's effect on population-level allele frequencies is negligible in the short term.
Answer
Purifying (negative) selection removes deleterious alleles from the population — alleles that reduce fitness are selected against and typically disappear over generations. "Monstrose" mutations (like crested cacti or abnormal cedar growth forms) are examples of somatic mutations that produce fitness-reducing phenotypes. They arise rarely, don't typically make it into the germline, and are filtered out by purifying selection, which is why they remain extremely rare in nature.
Answer
A mutation that confers a strong fitness advantage (positive selection) in combination with a small, isolated population would rapidly increase in frequency. For example: a new drought-resistance mutation arises in a small isolated tree population during a prolonged drought. Trees carrying the allele survive and reproduce better (positive selection). Because the population is small, even a few extra offspring make a large proportional difference. Limited migration prevents dilution of the allele, and over generations it can sweep to fixation.

§8 — Genetic Drift, Bottleneck & Founder Effect

Answer
Both involve a dramatic reduction to a small number of individuals, but the context differs. A bottleneck occurs when an existing population is suddenly reduced by a catastrophic event (disease, disaster) and then re-expands in the same location. A founder effect occurs when a small group of individuals colonizes a new territory and becomes isolated — the founding group's allele frequencies define the new population. Both result in reduced genetic diversity and allele frequencies that may differ substantially from the original population.

Both populations experienced independent founder effects. The Tay-Sachs allele that became common in each population arose as a separate mutation in a separate founding individual. Each population was subsequently isolated (French Canadians by language/religion from the British; Ashkenazi Jews by religious practice and culture), so each mutation rose in frequency within its own founding gene pool through drift, without being diluted by gene flow from other populations.
