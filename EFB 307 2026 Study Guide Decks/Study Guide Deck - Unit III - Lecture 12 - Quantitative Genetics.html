📊 Section 1 – Trait Types & Distributions
Qualitative vs. Quantitative Traits
Whether a trait is quantitative or qualitative depends on what is being observed

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Define qualitative and quantitative traits
  • Recognize the type of distribution each follows
  • Provide biological examples of each

🔷 Qualitative Traits

Fall into discrete categories. Follow a binomial distribution.


Examples: blood type (A, B, O, AB); presence/absence of trichomes in Glycine max (controlled by dominant gene Pb1)

🔶 Quantitative Traits

Are continuous. Follow a Gaussian (normal) distribution.


Examples: human height; lesion size caused by Cryphonectria parasitica in chestnut

Key definition: A phenotype is any characteristic that can be measured using some assay. The same biological attribute (e.g., disease resistance) can be measured as either qualitative or quantitative depending on the measurement used.
📊 Section 1 – Trait Types & Distributions
Defining a Distribution
The mean and variance or standard deviation characterize the shape of any distribution
  • Mean (μ) – the average value of all measurements
  • Median – the middle value (50th percentile)
  • Standard deviation (SD or σ) – describes the width of the distribution; approximately 68% of values fall within ±1 SD of the mean
  • Variance (σ²) – the square of the standard deviation; calculated as the average of squared distances from the mean (mean sum of squares)
σ² = variance = (standard deviation)²
Applied example: In a study of bud yaw angle across chestnut backcross generations, BC₃ trees had a mean of 17.3° and standard error of 1.16. With n ≈ 49 trees, σ = 1.16 × √49 = 8.12. This means ~68% of BC₃ trees fall between 9.18° and 25.42°.
Standard error vs. standard deviation: SE = σ/√n. SE describes uncertainty about the mean; SD describes spread of individual values.
🧬 Section 2 – Polygenic Inheritance & QTLs
Additive Genetic Variance & the Multiple-Gene Hypothesis
Continuous variation arises from many genes acting in Mendelian fashion

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Explain how multiple genes produce continuous phenotypic variation
  • Define additive alleles and polygenic inheritance
  • Estimate the number of polygenes using two methods

When alleles of many genes all contribute to a phenotype, more phenotypic categories can be observed and distinctions between categories become smaller — ultimately producing a continuous distribution.

  • Monogenic (1 gene): Clearly distinct phenotypic classes
  • Several genes: More categories, less distinction
  • Many genes (polygenic): Continuous phenotypes

🔑 Polygenic / Multigenic Trait

A quantitative phenotype controlled by more than one gene (polygenes) each behaving in Mendelian fashion. Alleles are additive — each copy contributes a fixed, equal amount to the phenotype.

Classic example (H. Nilsson-Ehle, 1909): Wheat kernel color. R = additive allele (contributes to redness); r = nonadditive allele. Crossing R₁R₁R₂R₂ (dark red) × r₁r₁r₂r₂ (white) → F₁ medium red → F₂ produces 5 phenotypic categories in a 1:4:6:4:1 ratio.
🧬 Section 2 – Polygenic Inheritance & QTLs
Estimating the Number of Polygenes
Two methods based on F₂ phenotypic class frequencies

Both methods assume alleles contribute equally and additively, with no environmental effect.

Method 1 — Extreme phenotype ratio

The proportion of F₂ offspring showing the most extreme phenotype equals 1/4ⁿ, where n = number of polygenes.

Formula: n = log(1/p) / log(4)

Example: If 1/16 of F₂ are extreme → n = log(16)/log(4) = 2

Method 2 — Number of phenotypic categories

The number of phenotypic bins in F₂ equals 2n + 1.

Formula: n = (bins − 1) / 2

Example: If there are 5 categories → n = (5 − 1)/2 = 2

⚠️ Assumptions: These methods only work when all alleles contribute equally and additively, and when the environment has no effect on phenotype — rarely fully met in real populations.
🧬 Section 2 – Polygenic Inheritance & QTLs
Quantitative Trait Loci (QTLs)
Genomic regions associated with quantitative phenotypic variation
  • A QTL is a region of DNA (a locus) containing genes that contribute to a complex, multifactorial, or quantitative trait
  • Multiple genes within a QTL may work together to determine phenotype
  • QTL genes may be co-inherited due to physical linkage on a chromosome
  • GWAS (Genome-Wide Association Study) is a powerful approach for identifying QTLs — a Manhattan plot displays statistical significance (–log₁₀ P) across chromosomes, with peaks indicating likely QTL locations
Forest Genetics context: QTL mapping is used to understand variation in traits relevant to forest management goals — diversity, conservation, productivity, resilience, and especially breeding for disease resistance.
Full-sib vs. Half-sib families (relevant to QTL studies in trees):
Full-sibs share both parents. Half-sibs share only one parent (typically the mother in open-pollination studies). Family structure is critical for partitioning genetic variance.
🧬 Section 2 – Polygenic Inheritance & QTLs
Major-Gene vs. Quantitative Resistance
The duality of qualitative and quantitative trait measurement

The same biological attribute — such as disease resistance — can be measured as either qualitative or quantitative, depending on the trait used:

  • Qualitative measurement: survival (0 = dead, 1 = alive) — a threshold trait
  • Quantitative measurement: time to death (years), severity index, lesion size

Major-Gene Resistance (MGR)

Controlled by a single dominant gene. Phenotype is largely discrete (resistant vs. susceptible). Example: Cr2 gene in Pinus monticola conferring hypersensitive reaction to Cronartium ribicola (white pine blister rust).

Quantitative Resistance (QR)

Controlled by many genes. Phenotype is continuous (a spectrum of resistance levels). Example: TACF backcross chestnut program — canker severity varies continuously across families.

Threshold trait: A qualitative phenotype (e.g., alive/dead) that is under polygenic control. The phenotype appears categorical, but the underlying inheritance is quantitative. Example: % survival of P. strobiformis families infected with C. ribicola varies continuously → quantitatively inherited.
🌿 Section 3 – Complex Traits
Complex (Multifactorial) Traits
Phenotype = f(Genotype, Environment)

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Define complex/multifactorial traits
  • Apply the P ~ f(G, E) model to real examples
  • Explain how environmental variation affects phenotypic distributions

A complex (multifactorial) trait is one whose phenotype is shaped by both multiple genes and the environment — the classic "nature and nurture" interaction.

  • Concept emerged alongside Mendelian genetics, developed by Sir Ronald Fisher
  • Most quantitative traits are likely complex traits
  • Examples: human height, tree diameter, seed weight, disease severity
P ~ f (G, E)
Example 1: Port-Orford cedar (Chamaecyparis lawsoniana) tree diameter measured at two sites (Battle Axe vs. Meis) planted the same year. Mean DBH differed by ~10 cm between sites, demonstrating strong environmental influence on the same genotypes.
Example 2: Seed weight in maritime pine (Pinus pinaster) — families show consistent rank order across two sites (parallel lines in reaction norm plot), indicating both genetic differences among families and environmental effects across sites.
📐 Section 4 – Heritability
What Is Heritability?
Partitioning phenotypic variance into genetic and environmental components

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Define heritability and state its range
  • Explain VP = VG + VE and what each component represents
  • Distinguish broad-sense (H²) from narrow-sense (h²) heritability
  • Describe key assumptions and limitations of heritability estimates
  • Calculate heritability from given variance components

Heritability is the proportion of total phenotypic variance in a population that can be explained by genetic differences among individuals.

0 ≤ H² ≤ 1

H² = 0

All observed variation is due to the environment. Genetic differences among individuals do not contribute to phenotypic differences.

H² = 1

All observed variation is due to genetic differences. The environment has no effect on phenotypic variation in this population.

Critical applications: Heritability is fundamental to medicine (predicting disease risk), plant and animal breeding (predicting selection response), and conservation genetics (assessing adaptive potential).
📐 Section 4 – Heritability
Variance Components: VP = VG + VE

To estimate heritability you need: (1) knowledge of genotype, genetic relatedness, or genetic background, and (2) a way to account for environmental factors across individuals.
📐 Section 4 – Heritability
Broad-Sense vs. Narrow-Sense Heritability
Two ways to partition genetic contributions to phenotypic variance

Broad-Sense Heritability (H²)

Accounts for all forms of genetic variance — additive, dominance, and epistatic effects.


H² = VG / VP

Uses the total genetic variance in the numerator. Less useful for predicting offspring performance.

Narrow-Sense Heritability (h²)

Accounts for additive genetic variance only — ignores dominance and epistasis.


h² = VA / VP

More useful for predicting selection response and breeding programs, since additive effects are transmitted to offspring.

Key distinction: h² ≤ H² always, because VA ≤ VG. When dominance and epistasis are negligible, h² ≈ H².
📐 Section 4 – Heritability
Assumptions & Limitations of Heritability
Heritability is population- and environment-specific — not a universal property of a trait
  • Heritability does not tell you how much of a trait is "genetically determined" in any absolute sense
  • It only applies to the specific population and environment being studied
  • If environmental variation increases → VE increases → H² decreases
  • If environmental variation is reduced (e.g., controlled greenhouse) → VE decreases → H² increases
Example: Heritability of systolic blood pressure varies widely across ethnic groups and studies (from ~0.16 to ~0.66), demonstrating that heritability estimates are not stable across populations.
Example (disease resistance): Heritability of blister rust resistance is higher in controlled greenhouse inoculations (reduced VE) than in passive field infection (high VE).
Practical implication: Always report the population, environment, and method when stating a heritability estimate. Two studies measuring "the same trait" may yield very different values.

Question 1

A researcher measures whether individual pea plants produce round or wrinkled seeds. Is this a qualitative or quantitative trait? What type of distribution would you expect?

This is a qualitative trait — it falls into discrete categories (round vs. wrinkled). It would follow a binomial distribution, not a continuous one, because the phenotype is controlled by a single gene with dominant/recessive alleles.

Question 2

In a population of American chestnuts, you measure canker lesion area after fungal infection. What type of trait is this, and what distribution would you expect? What does the mean and standard deviation tell you?

Lesion area is a quantitative trait that follows a Gaussian (normal) distribution. The mean (μ) tells you the average lesion size in the population. The standard deviation (σ) describes the spread — approximately 68% of individuals will have lesion sizes within ±1 SD of the mean, reflecting the natural variation in resistance across the population.

Question 3

In a chestnut experiment, the bud yaw angle in BC₃ trees has a mean of 17.3° and a standard error of 1.16 based on ~49 trees. What is the standard deviation of this sample?

SE = σ/√n, so σ = SE × √n = 1.16 × √49 = 1.16 × 7 = 8.12°. This means ~68% of BC₃ trees have bud yaw angles between 9.18° and 25.42°.
❓ Section 5 – Review Questions
Section 2: Polygenic Inheritance & QTLs

Question 4

In an F₂ cross, you observe that 1/64 of offspring show the most extreme phenotype. Using Method 1, how many polygenes control this trait?

Using Method 1: 1/4ⁿ = 1/64 → 4ⁿ = 64 → n = log(64)/log(4) = 3 polygenes. You can verify: 4³ = 64. ✓

Question 5

An F₂ population for a wheat color cross shows 7 distinct phenotypic categories. Using Method 2, how many polygenes control kernel color?

Using Method 2: 2n + 1 = 7 → 2n = 6 → n = 3 polygenes. Remember the key assumptions: equal additive contributions per allele, and no environmental effect.

Question 6

What is the difference between a QTL and a single gene controlling a trait? How is GWAS used to identify QTLs?

A QTL (Quantitative Trait Locus) is a chromosomal region containing one or more genes that collectively contribute to a quantitative trait — unlike a single gene controlling a qualitative trait. In GWAS, thousands of genetic markers (SNPs) are tested across the genome for statistical association with the trait. Peaks in a Manhattan plot (high –log₁₀ P values) indicate QTL regions where genetic variation correlates with phenotypic variation.

Question 7

Tree survival after blister rust infection (0 = dead, 1 = alive) appears to be a qualitative trait. Yet % survival varies continuously among families. How do you reconcile this?

This is a threshold trait — the individual-level phenotype is categorical (alive or dead), but the underlying genetic control is polygenic. Because many genes influence susceptibility, different families carry different numbers of resistance alleles, producing a continuous range of family-level survival rates. The phenotype appears qualitative at the individual level, but the inheritance is quantitative.

Section 3 & 4: Complex Traits & Heritability

Question 8

Two genetically identical Port-Orford cedar trees are planted at Battle Axe (cold, high elevation) vs. Meis (warmer site). After the same number of years, the Meis tree has a DBH nearly twice that of the Battle Axe tree. What does this tell you about tree diameter as a trait?

Tree diameter is a complex (multifactorial) trait described by P ~ f(G, E). Even with the same genotype, different environments produce dramatically different phenotypes. This illustrates that the environment (E) makes a large contribution to phenotypic variance (VE), and that heritability estimates for DBH will be lower in heterogeneous field conditions than in controlled environments.

Question 9 — Heritability Calculation (Rabbit Ear Length)

Researchers measure ear length in a rabbit population and find total phenotypic variance VP = 100. Environmental variance VE = 12. (a) What is VG? (b) What is H²? (c) Is this broad- or narrow-sense heritability?

(a) VG = VP − VE = 100 − 12 = 88
(b) H² = VG/VP = 88/100 = 0.88 — a high heritability, meaning 88% of the variation in ear length is due to genetic differences.
(c) This is broad-sense heritability (H²), because VG includes all genetic effects (additive, dominance, epistasis). To calculate narrow-sense h², you would need VA (additive genetic variance only).

Question 10 — Heritability Calculation (Disease)

For a disease in a population, VG = 45 and VE = 25. (a) What is VP? (b) What is H²? (c) If the study is moved to a controlled lab setting that reduces VE to 5, what happens to H²?

(a) VP = VG + VE = 45 + 25 = 70
(b) H² = 45/70 ≈ 0.64
(c) New VP = 45 + 5 = 50; new H² = 45/50 = 0.90. Reducing environmental variance increases H² — this is why controlled experiments yield higher heritability estimates, and why heritability is environment-specific.

Question 11 — Conceptual

A news article claims "height has a heritability of 0.8, meaning 80% of your height is determined by your genes." Is this interpretation correct? Explain.

This is a common misinterpretation. Heritability of 0.8 does not mean 80% of any individual's height is genetic. It means that in the specific population and environment studied, 80% of the variation in height among individuals can be attributed to genetic differences. If everyone were raised in identical environments, heritability would be higher; if there were extreme environmental differences (malnutrition vs. optimal nutrition), heritability would be lower. Heritability is a population statistic, not a property of individuals.
