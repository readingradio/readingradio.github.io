Lecture 19 – Gene Regulation & Analysis (& CRISPR)

EFB 307 | Key topics: Operons, gene networks, ChIP, forward/reverse genetics, CRISPR, classic Nobel-prize screens

This lecture ties together how genes are turned on and off in prokaryotes, how those regulatory interactions form networks, and how geneticists use screens and tools like CRISPR to figure out what genes do.

Inducible

Expression triggered/stopped by a signal

Constitutive

Always "on" — no signal needed

Operon

Cluster of genes sharing a single promoter

Operator

DNA sequence within promoter where repressor binds

Polycistronic

Single mRNA encoding multiple polypeptides

Auxotroph

Organism that cannot synthesize a required molecule

Corepressor

Small molecule that activates a repressor

Effector gene

Gene encoding a functional protein — endpoint of a network

Q1. The trp operon is transcribed when tryptophan is absent. Is this positive or negative regulation, and is tryptophan acting as an inducer or corepressor?

This is negative regulation. A repressor protein controls the operon by binding the operator to block transcription. Tryptophan acts as a corepressor — it binds the Trp repressor, causing a conformational change that allows the repressor–Trp complex to bind the operator, shutting off transcription. When Trp is absent, the repressor can't bind and transcription proceeds freely.

Q2. A mutation in the trp operator causes constitutive expression. Would this mutation be complemented in trans? Explain why or why not.

No, it cannot be complemented in trans. The operator is a cis-regulatory element — it only controls transcription of genes on the same DNA molecule. Introducing a functional operator on a plasmid does nothing for the mutant chromosome's operator. Contrast this with a trpR mutation (no repressor protein): because the repressor is a diffusible molecule, a functional copy on a plasmid can complement the defect in trans.

Q3. Describe the two regulatory mechanisms that control the lac operon and state which sugar drives each one.

1. Negative regulation (catabolite repression) — controlled by glucose: When glucose is present it inhibits adenyl cyclase, so no cAMP is made. Without cAMP, CAP cannot bind the promoter, and transcription is low. When glucose is absent, cAMP accumulates, cAMP–CAP binds the promoter, and RNA polymerase is recruited → high transcription.

2. Positive regulation (de-repression) — controlled by lactose: When lactose is present, allolactose forms and binds the lac repressor, preventing the repressor from binding the operator. When lactose is absent, the repressor occupies the operator and blocks transcription.

Q4. What is trp attenuation, and what role do hairpin structures play?

Attenuation is a second layer of trp operon control that occurs within the leader (promoter) region upstream of trpE. The leader mRNA can fold into two alternative hairpins depending on ribosome position:

Tryptophan abundant: The ribosome reads through the trp codons in Region 1. Regions 3+4 form a terminator hairpin → RBS is blocked → no further transcription.

Tryptophan scarce: The ribosome stalls on trp codons, leaving Region 1 occupied. Regions 2+3 form an anti-terminator hairpin → Region 4 and RBS remain open → transcription continues → more Trp made.

Q5. Distinguish forward genetics from reverse genetics. Give one example of each from the lecture.

Forward genetics: Start with unknown genes, apply random mutagenesis, screen for a phenotype of interest, then identify the gene. Requires a model organism, mutants, and a selection/assay. Example: Beadle & Tatum (Neurospora arginine auxotrophs), Jacob & Monod (E. coli lac mutants), Nüsslein-Volhard & Wieschaus (Drosophila segmentation).

Reverse genetics: Start with a known gene (or hypothesis about gene function), mutate it specifically, and observe the phenotype. Requires a prior hypothesis and an assay. Example: Using CRISPR to knock out a specific gene whose function you suspect based on its ortholog in another species.

Q6. Walk through how CRISPR-Cas9 is used in a genome editing experiment. What happens if no donor template is provided vs. when one is provided?

1. Engineer a Cas9 expression plasmid and a sgRNA expression plasmid (contains a 20 nt sequence complementary to your target + tracrRNA).
2. Introduce both into mammalian cell culture (mammalian cells because they have robust HDR machinery).
3. The sgRNA guides Cas9 to the target; Cas9 binds at the PAM sequence and cuts both strands → double-strand break.

No donor template → NHEJ: error-prone repair creates indels → frameshift → gene silenced. Used in whole-genome screens.

With donor template → HDR: homology-directed repair inserts the donor sequence precisely at the cut site. Used for specific edits (corrections, insertions, tagging).

Q7. In the Jacob & Monod trans-complementation test, what is the key difference between a lacZ mutation and a lacOc mutation in terms of complementation?

lacZ mutation (enzyme defect): Can be complemented in trans. Introducing a plasmid with functional lacZ restores β-galactosidase production — the enzyme is a diffusible product that can act on the chromosome's genes. The operon becomes inducible again.

lacOc mutation (operator defect): Cannot be complemented in trans. The operator is a cis-acting DNA sequence; it only controls genes physically adjacent to it on the same chromosome. A functional operator on a plasmid doesn't fix the mutant chromosome's operator, so the chromosome still shows constitutive transcription.

Q8. Describe three types of embryonic segmentation mutations found in Drosophila by Nüsslein-Volhard, Wieschaus, and Lewis, and explain the phenotype associated with each.

Gap genes (e.g., Krüppel): Entire contiguous groups of segments are missing from the larva. Kr mutants lack thoracic and anterior abdominal segments.

Pair-rule genes (e.g., even-skipped / eve): Every other segment is deleted, halving the total number of segments. eve mutants lack the even-numbered segments.

Maternal genes (e.g., bicoid): Anterior–posterior patterning is disrupted. bicoid mutants lack head and thorax — the anterior end develops as a mirror-image of the posterior (two tails).

Also: segment polarity genes (orientation within each segment) and Hox genes (identity of segments).
