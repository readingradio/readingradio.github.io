Section 1
Defining a Population

What Is a Population?

A population is a group of organisms that:

  • Can exchange genetic material
  • Occupy a specific place
  • At a specific time
  • With a boundary (fixed or flexible)
Effective population (Ne) includes only individuals that can breed and exchange genetic information — often smaller than the census (actual) population size.

Why Population Genetics?

The Problem

Populations can range from thousands to hundreds of thousands of individuals. Tracking every cross is impossible.

The Solution

Instead of looking at crosses, look at outcomes. Track how allele frequencies change over time across the population.

Real-World Applications

Conservation
Assessing genetic diversity and extinction risk
Evolution
Tracking adaptive changes across generations
Breeding
Diversity, adaptation in managed species
Anthropology
Human migrations, ancestry, history

Polymorphic Loci & Haplotypes

Polymorphic Locus
A locus with more than one allele, where each allele occurs at frequency > 1%
Haplotype
A version of a polymorphic locus — e.g., a block of SNPs. Analogous to an allele at a larger scale
Haplotype Network
Diagram connecting haplotypes. Pie size = # samples; branch nodes = mutational steps between haplotypes
Structure Plot
Bar chart where each bar = one individual. Colors show the proportion of ancestry from each haplotype group
Reading a haplotype network: Pie colors show geographic origin of samples in that haplotype. Pie size shows how many samples share it. Each branch node = one mutational change between haplotypes.
Section 2
The Gene Pool

Key Concepts

Gene Pool
The total diversity of all genes and alleles present across all individuals in a population
Allele Frequency
How common a particular allele is in the gene pool — measured at the gamete level
Genotype Frequency
How common each combination of alleles (AA, Aa, aa) is among individuals in the population

Allele Frequencies: p and q

For a locus with two alleles (A and a):

p = frequency of allele A (in gametes)
q = frequency of allele a (in gametes)

p + q = 1
All alleles must account for 100% of the gene pool
Example: If 70 out of 100 gametes carry allele A, then p = 0.70 and q = 0.30.

Genotype Frequencies

Every individual in a diploid population is one of three genotype classes:

AA
Homozygous dominant — carries two copies of allele A
Aa
Heterozygous — carries one copy of each allele (Aa = aA, same genotype)
aa
Homozygous recessive — carries two copies of allele a
freq(AA) + freq(Aa) + freq(aa) = 1
Important distinction: Allele frequencies are measured in gametes; genotype frequencies are measured in individuals. These are related but not the same thing.
Section 3
Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium

HWE as a Null Model

HWE is the null hypothesis of population genetics. It describes what we expect if nothing interesting is happening — if the population is not evolving.

A population is in HWE when:
1. Genotype frequencies can be predicted from allele frequencies
2. Allele and genotype frequencies do not change generation to generation

The HWE Equation

p² + 2pq + q² = 1
(p + q)² = 1
GenotypeFrequencyMeaning
AA p² % of individuals homozygous dominant
Aa 2pq % of individuals heterozygous (Aa = aA, so ×2)
aa q² % of individuals homozygous recessive
Why the "2" in 2pq? Because Aa and aA are the same genotype — there are two ways to be heterozygous (get A from mom + a from dad, or vice versa).

Worked Example

A population has marker Z at frequency 25%. What are the allele and genotype frequencies under HWE?

1
Set up allele frequencies
p (freq. of Z) = 0.25  |  q (freq. of z) = 1 − 0.25 = 0.75
Check: 0.25 + 0.75 = 1 ✓
2
Calculate genotype frequencies
ZZ (p²) = 0.25² = 0.0625
zz (q²) = 0.75² = 0.5625
Zz (2pq) = 2 × 0.25 × 0.75 = 0.3750
3
Verify they sum to 1
0.0625 + 0.5625 + 0.3750 = 1.0 ✓

5 Assumptions of HWE

  • 1
    Random mating
    All individuals equally likely to mate with any other individual
  • 2
    No mutation
    No new alleles are introduced into the gene pool
  • 3
    No migration
    No gene flow into or out of the population
  • 4
    Large population size
    Prevents random allele frequency shifts (genetic drift)
  • 5
    No natural selection
    All alleles are neutral — none confers a fitness advantage or disadvantage
These 5 assumptions define a population that is not evolving. Any violation signals that something biological is happening.
Section 4
Deviations (Violations) from HWE

Why Violations Matter

HWE is a null hypothesis. When observed frequencies differ from expected, it means a biological force is acting on the population.

Genotype frequency changes only
Allele frequencies stay the same → population at equilibrium (not truly evolving)
Allele frequency changes (Δq > 0)
Alleles are gained or lost over time → population is evolving

HWE Assumptions vs. Violations

Assumption Violation Impact on Population Type
Random mating Non-random / assortative mating Changes genotype ratios only; stratification or shifts in heterozygosity Equilibrium
No mutation Mutation occurs New alleles arise in the gene pool Evolves
No migration Gene flow occurs Blocked migration → populations diverge; migration → populations converge Evolves
Large population Genetic drift Allele frequencies change at random; small pops risk fixation or loss Evolves
No differential reproduction Natural selection Varies by selection type (directional, stabilizing, disruptive) Evolves

Key Concepts in Violations

Genetic Drift
Random changes in allele frequency due to small population size. Can lead to fixation (allele reaches 100%) or loss (0%)
Fixation
When one allele reaches a frequency of 1.0 (100%) and all other alleles are eliminated
Neutral Variation
Alleles that have no fitness effect — their frequency changes are driven by drift, not selection
Selection
Differential survival/reproduction based on genotype. Types: directional, stabilizing, disruptive

§3 — Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium

Answer
1. Random mating — individuals pair randomly with respect to genotype
2. No mutation — no new alleles are introduced
3. No migration — no gene flow in or out
4. Large population — minimizes the effects of random drift
5. No natural selection — all alleles are neutral with equal fitness
Answer
HWE describes a baseline expectation — what allele and genotype frequencies should look like if no evolutionary forces are acting. It is a null model in the statistical sense: we assume nothing interesting is happening, and then test whether real data deviate significantly from this expectation. Deviations indicate some biological process (selection, drift, gene flow, etc.) is at work.
Answer
Nothing — allele and genotype frequencies remain constant from generation to generation. This is the defining property of equilibrium. Even after one generation of random mating, if all 5 assumptions hold, frequencies stabilize and remain unchanged indefinitely.

§4 — HWE Violations

Answer
Genotype frequencies only (population still at equilibrium): Non-random mating (assortative mating) — it shuffles genotype combinations without changing which alleles exist in the pool.

Allele frequencies change (population evolves): Mutation (new alleles), migration (gene flow), genetic drift (random loss/fixation), and natural selection (differential survival) all directly change what alleles are present and how common they are.
Answer
Genetic drift is the random change in allele frequencies that occurs because reproduction involves sampling — and sampling error is larger in small samples. In a small population, chance events (who survives, who reproduces) can dramatically shift allele frequencies, potentially leading to fixation (an allele reaches 100%) or loss (an allele disappears). In large populations, these random fluctuations average out and have little net effect.
Answer
Migration has two contrasting effects: if it is blocked or prevented, populations become isolated and diverge over time, accumulating different alleles. If gene flow occurs, alleles are shared between populations, making them more genetically similar to one another. This is why isolated populations (like island species) tend to be genetically distinct, while connected populations tend to converge.
Answer
The random mating assumption is likely being violated. Non-random mating (e.g., inbreeding or assortative mating) tends to increase homozygosity and decrease heterozygosity relative to HWE expectations — without changing allele frequencies. This is a genotype-only violation.
