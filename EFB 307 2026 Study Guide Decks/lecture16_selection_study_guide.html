Selection and Fitness

Fitness = the ability to survive to reproducing age and successfully reproduce. Traits that improve the fitness of a genotype are called adaptive.

The last HWE assumption is that all individuals have an equal chance at reproducing. Darwin's natural selection violates this in real populations through differential reproduction.

Section i
Directional Selection
One allele is consistently favored; the population shifts toward one phenotypic extreme
Directional Selection — Slide Deck

What is Directional Selection?

Directional selection occurs when one allele consistently confers a fitness advantage over alternative alleles. Over generations, that allele increases in frequency — possibly reaching fixation (p = 1).

The population mean phenotype shifts in one direction over time. Think of the distribution curve sliding left or right.

Key features: allele frequency change is directional and monotonic (keeps going the same way over time).

Classic Example: Allele A under Positive Selection

Starting from a heterozygous parent (Aa) with p = 0.5:

GenerationFrequency of A allele (p)
F₁0.5
F₂0.625
F₃0.78
F₄0.89
AA individuals reproduce more successfully → AA offspring proportion grows each generation → A allele becomes increasingly common

Human Example: Lactase Persistence (LCT)

Normally, the lactase enzyme (LCT) stops being produced after infancy. Lactase persistence — continuing LCT production into adulthood — is a derived mutation.

Some human populations show strong positive selection for lactase persistence. Why? Populations that were traditionally pastoralists (herding cattle, goats, camels) had a major nutritional advantage from being able to digest milk as adults.

The frequency of LCT persistence is highest in Northern Europe and pastoral African populations — precisely where livestock herding was historically most common.

Peppered Moth: Directional Selection in Action

Before the Industrial Revolution: light-colored moths were better camouflaged on pale lichen-covered trees → selected for.

During the Industrial Revolution: soot darkened trees → dark-colored moths became better camouflaged and were selected for.

The phenotype distribution shifted from predominantly light to predominantly dark — a textbook example of directional selection driven by a changing environment.

This also illustrates that the direction of selection can reverse if the environment changes (after pollution controls, light moths increased again).

Section ii
Balancing / Stabilizing Selection
Intermediate phenotypes or heterozygotes have highest fitness — variation is maintained
Balancing & Stabilizing Selection — Slide Deck

Balancing Selection: Heterozygote Advantage

In balancing selection, the heterozygote (Aa) has higher fitness than either homozygote. This is called overdominance or heterozygote advantage.

Result: Both alleles are maintained in the population at stable equilibrium frequencies. Neither allele is driven to fixation.

The allele frequency still increases over generations, but more slowly than directional selection — and stabilizes rather than going to fixation.

Classic Example: Sickle Cell Anemia (HbS)

GenotypePhenotypeFitness in malaria zones
HbS/HbS (homozygous)Sickle cell diseaseLow ✗
Hb/Hb (wild type)Normal hemoglobinSusceptible to malaria ✗
Hb/HbS (heterozygous)CarrierResistant to malaria + no sickle cell ✓
The HbS allele is deleterious in homozygotes but advantageous in heterozygotes in malaria-endemic regions. This is why it persists — natural selection maintains it through heterozygote advantage.

Stabilizing Selection

Closely related to balancing selection — but operating on quantitative / continuous traits. The intermediate phenotype is favored; both extremes are selected against.

Classic example — Robin clutch size: Robins typically lay 4 eggs. Larger clutches produce malnourished chicks; smaller clutches mean fewer or no viable offspring. The intermediate size (4) is optimal.

Effect on the distribution: the bell curve gets taller and narrower — variance decreases but the mean stays the same.

Haplotype Length as Evidence of Selection

Haplotypes shorten over generations due to recombination breaking up linked SNPs. Therefore:

A long haplotype indicates: (1) recent origin of the mutation, and (2) strong selection — the region hasn't had time to be broken up by recombination because selection spread it so fast.

SNPs that remained linked on a common haplotype must have experienced strong selection. QTLs for quantitative traits can be maintained as haplotype blocks too.

EDAR Variant — Han Chinese Example

A variant in the EDAR gene is nearly fixed (~90% frequency) among Han Chinese but absent in other populations.

EDAR affects: hair thickness, sweat gland production, and tooth shape.

The EDAR haplotype is very long → positive selection happened recently. The allele spread so quickly through the population that recombination hasn't had time to shorten the surrounding haplotype.

Section iii
Disruptive / Diversifying Selection
Extreme phenotypes are favored; intermediate phenotypes are selected against — can drive speciation
Disruptive Selection — Slide Deck

What is Disruptive Selection?

The opposite of stabilizing selection. Both extreme phenotypes have higher fitness than the intermediate phenotype.

Result: The population distribution becomes bimodal — two peaks, one at each extreme, with a valley in the middle. Over time, this can lead to speciation if the two groups become reproductively isolated.

Also called diversifying selection because it increases phenotypic diversity within a population.

Example: Rabbit Coat Color

In a rocky environment, gray rabbits (blending with rock) and Himalayan (gray-and-white) rabbits (blending with patches of snow and rock) are better camouflaged than white rabbits (stand out against rock).

White rabbits (intermediate between dark gray and Himalayan pattern) are most visible to predators → selected against. The two extreme color morphs survive better → bimodal distribution.

Example: Galápagos Finches — Beak Depth

In environments with two distinct food sources (small soft seeds and large hard seeds), birds with small beaks (good for small seeds) and birds with large beaks (good for hard seeds) both succeed.

Birds with intermediate beak depth are less efficient at either food source → selected against. Over time, the beak depth distribution becomes bimodal (8mm and 21mm peaks), with a trough in between (~13mm).

This is a real-world example that shows how disruptive selection can drive divergence and eventually speciation.

Section iv
Population Structure: FST and QST
Quantifying genetic differentiation among populations and comparing neutral vs. adaptive divergence

FST — The Fixation Index

FST = 1 – HS / HT
HS = average heterozygosity within subpopulations  |  HT = heterozygosity of the total population

FST ranges from 0 to 1 and quantifies how genetically different subpopulations are from each other.

FST valueInterpretation
= 0.0No genetic differences between populations — all share the same allele frequencies
0 – 0.05Very small/insignificant differences
0.05 – 0.15Moderate genetic differences
0.15 – 0.25Large genetic differences; may have formed differently
0.25 – 0.5Very large differences; populations quite distinct
0.5 – 1.0Stage of subspecies; genetic similarity near zero
= 1.0Complete fixation of different alleles; effectively different species

QST — Quantitative Trait Differentiation

QST is the analog of FST but for quantitative (polygenic) traits rather than neutral molecular markers. It measures how much more (or less) differentiated a trait is between populations compared to neutral expectation.

Comparing FST to QST reveals whether selection is driving trait differentiation:
ComparisonInterpretation
FST = QSTTrait differentiation consistent with genetic drift alone — no evidence for selection
FST < QSTDivergent selection — traits are MORE differentiated than neutral markers → local adaptation
FST > QSTStabilizing selection — similar phenotypes maintained across populations despite genetic differentiation

Applied Example: FST < QST — Climate Adaptation in Trees

When QST exceeds FST for climate-related traits (e.g., bud set timing, cold hardiness), it indicates that local populations have adapted their quantitative traits to local climates more than would be expected from neutral drift alone.

This "genomic offset" approach is used to quantify how mismatched a population is to its climate — important for conservation genetics and predicting how species will respond to climate change.
Section v
Detecting Selection: Jukes-Cantor (dN/dS)
Comparing synonymous and non-synonymous substitution rates to infer selection from genome sequences
Jukes-Cantor Model — Slide Deck

Key Definitions

TermDefinition
Synonymous substitution (dS)Change in the 3rd base of a codon that does NOT change the amino acid produced (silent mutation — "synonymous" with original)
Non-synonymous substitution (dN)Change in a codon that DOES result in a different amino acid being produced (changes protein structure/function)
The Jukes-Cantor model compares dN/dS to determine whether a gene is under positive selection, neutral, or negative/purifying selection.

Interpreting dN/dS

dN/dS ratioInterpretationSelection type
= 1Non-synonymous and synonymous rates are equal; mutation is neither advantageous nor deleteriousNeutral
> 1More non-synonymous substitutions than expected — protein changes are being retained → mutation is advantageousPositive selection
< 1Fewer non-synonymous substitutions — protein changes are being removed → most mutations are deleteriousNegative (purifying) selection
Most genes have dN/dS < 1 — most mutations are deleterious and are purged by selection. Only a small subset of genes or regions show dN/dS > 1 (adaptive evolution).

How It Works: Comparing Two Genomes

Two genome sequences are aligned. At each codon, substitutions are classified as synonymous or non-synonymous.

The dN/dS ratio is calculated for each region of the genome. Regions where dN/dS spikes above 1 indicate a locus where mutations have been positively selected.

This lets us scan entire genomes for signatures of selection — without knowing in advance which genes might be under selection. The genome sequence itself tells us where selection has acted.

Connection to HWE: When Would dN/dS ≈ 1?

dN/dS = 1 (neutrality) corresponds to the HWE assumption that alleles are neutral (Δq = 0). If mutations have no effect on fitness, non-synonymous changes accumulate at the same rate as synonymous ones.

Departures from dN/dS = 1 are molecular evidence of selection — exactly the kind of HWE violation we study in population genetics.

dN/dS > 1 → positive selection → adaptive evolution (the "useful" mutations are being kept)
dN/dS < 1 → purifying selection → functional constraint (the "harmful" mutations are being removed)

Section vi
Frequency-Dependent Selection
Fitness of an allele depends on how common it is — drives host-pathogen coevolution and the Red Queen hypothesis
Frequency-Dependent Selection — Slide Deck

What is Frequency-Dependent Selection?

The fitness of an allele changes depending on how common it is in the population. This is distinct from other types of selection where fitness is fixed.

Key tradeoff: Carrying a resistance allele always has a cost (e.g., growth vs. defense). The benefit of the allele depends on the pathogen population — which in turn depends on the frequency of the resistance allele in hosts.

Result: Allele frequencies oscillate over time rather than going to fixation. Neither allele fully wins.

Host-Pathogen Coevolution: The Cycle

  1. An infrequent resistance allele (a) confers protection against the pathogen
  2. Resistance allele increases in frequency (selected for)
  3. Pathogen evolves (mutates) to infect the now-common resistance allele
  4. Original allele (AA) now confers resistance to the new pathogen → increases in frequency again
  5. Cycle repeats → oscillating allele frequencies
This creates the classic oscillating pattern seen in the host allele A vs. parasite allele A frequency graph — both cycling over hundreds of generations.

Gene-for-Gene Interactions in Plants & Pathogens

Host plants carry Resistance (R) genes. Pathogens carry Avirulence (Avr) genes that produce elicitors recognized by R proteins.

Host R genePathogen Avr gene (AA/Aa)Pathogen avr gene (aa)
RR/Rr (resistant)RESISTANCE (incompatible reaction)SUSCEPTIBILITY (compatible reaction)
rr (susceptible)SUSCEPTIBILITY (compatible reaction)SUSCEPTIBILITY (compatible reaction)
Resistance only occurs when the host has a functional R gene AND the pathogen has the matching Avr gene. This is the gene-for-gene model.

Real Example: White Pine Blister Rust

Cronartium ribicola is a pathogen that cycles between Pinus monticola (western white pine) and Ribes (currants/gooseberries).

The major resistance gene Cr2 is distributed unevenly across Pacific Northwest populations. Where the virulent strain (vcr2) of the fungus is common, the Cr2 resistance allele is more frequent.

This geographic mosaic of host resistance and pathogen virulence is a real-world snapshot of ongoing frequency-dependent coevolution.

Consequences of Frequency-Dependent Selection

  • Leads to stable variation in resistance within a population — not fixed at 0% or 100%
  • Some individuals "freeload" — they lack costly resistance alleles but benefit when pathogens are rare
  • Promotes diversity and coevolution between interacting species
  • Explains why parasites often evolve toward lower virulence over time (killing hosts too fast = less transmission)
Key principle: You can't win forever. Whatever allele is common becomes a target for pathogens to adapt to. This keeps genetic variation alive in the population.

Section vii
Sexual Selection, Kin Selection & Why Diversity Exists
Additional selection mechanisms and the big-picture explanation for maintained genetic variation

v. Sexual Selection 🦚

Selection for traits that increase mating success, not just survival. Can sometimes favor traits that reduce survival (e.g., peacock tail — conspicuous to predators but attractive to females).

Sexual selection can drive extreme dimorphism between sexes and rapid trait evolution, even when the trait reduces survival fitness.

vi. Kin Selection 🐒

Selection that favors inclusive fitness — individuals behave in ways that increase the reproductive success of relatives who share their genes.

Hamilton's rule: altruism evolves when r·B > C (relatedness × benefit > cost). Explains eusociality in bees, wasps, ants, and cooperative behaviors in primates.

🔴 The Red Queen Hypothesis

"Here, you see, it takes all the running you can do, to keep in the same place." — The Red Queen, Through the Looking Glass, Lewis Carroll

Hosts and pathogens coevolve in an arms race. To maintain resistance against ever-adapting pathogens, hosts must continually evolve — just to stay in the same place (not go extinct).

The Red Queen hypothesis also predicts that sexual reproduction and mechanisms that increase genetic diversity are favored — because diversity makes it harder for pathogens to find a universal attack strategy.

Why Does Genetic Diversity Persist?

  • The favorable allele may be increasing but hasn't fixed yet — insufficient time
  • Genetic drift and migration counteract selection
  • Fitness of an individual depends on many loci simultaneously
  • The environment is not constant — a favorable allele now may not be favorable later
  • Multiple types of selection operating at once (frequency-dependent, stabilizing, etc.)
  • Constant coevolutionary interactions keep allele frequencies oscillating
Core relationship: Phenotype ≈ Genotype × Environment → Fitness ≈ Phenotype × Environment → Selection ≈ Fitness → Changes in allele & genotype frequencies

Bark & Ambrosia Beetles: Applying Multiple Selection Types

A rich case study for applying multiple selection frameworks:

  • Haplodiploidy + kin selection: In haplodiploid Xyleborini, sisters share 75% of genes → strong kin selection pressure toward eusociality and cooperation
  • Sexual selection: In polyandrous species, male parental care and repeated copulation keep males engaged in rearing — could evolve through kin selection (males are related to offspring) or sexual selection (male investment signals quality)
  • Disruptive/sexual selection in Gnathotrupes: Highly diverse declivity and facial hair morphology — likely driven by assortative mating + sexual selection in single-file tunnels where face-to-declivity contact occurs between mates

Review Questions

Q1
What is the last HWE assumption, and how does selection violate it?
The last HWE assumption is that all alleles are neutral (Δq = 0) — meaning all individuals have an equal chance of reproducing. Selection violates this through differential reproduction: genotypes with higher fitness reproduce more, changing allele frequencies across generations (Δq ≠ 0).
Q2
Compare and contrast directional, stabilizing, and disruptive selection in terms of what is favored and the effect on the phenotype distribution.
Directional: One extreme phenotype is favored → the mean shifts in one direction, variance roughly unchanged.

Stabilizing: The intermediate phenotype is favored → the mean stays the same but variance decreases (distribution narrows/sharpens).

Disruptive: Both extremes are favored, intermediate is selected against → bimodal distribution forms, variance increases. Can lead to speciation.
Q3
Explain heterozygote advantage using sickle cell anemia as your example. Why does the HbS allele persist in malaria-endemic populations?
The HbS allele causes sickle cell disease in homozygotes (HbS/HbS) — a fitness disadvantage. However, wild-type homozygotes (Hb/Hb) are susceptible to malaria. Heterozygotes (Hb/HbS) are resistant to malaria AND don't have sickle cell disease — giving them the highest fitness in malaria zones. Because the heterozygote is the most fit, both alleles are maintained at stable frequencies — this is balancing selection via heterozygote advantage.
Q4
What does a long haplotype block indicate about the history of selection on that region of the genome?
A long haplotype indicates: (1) recent origin — the mutation arose recently, so recombination hasn't had much time to break up surrounding SNPs, and (2) strong positive selection — the allele swept through the population so rapidly that nearby linked SNPs "hitchhiked" along before recombination could separate them. The EDAR gene variant in Han Chinese is a prime example: nearly fixed at 90%, with a very long haplotype indicating recent rapid positive selection.
Q5
What does FST = 1 – HS/HT measure? If HS = 0.2 and HT = 0.4, calculate FST and interpret it.
FST measures genetic differentiation among populations. A high FST means populations are very genetically distinct; low FST means they're similar.

Calculation: FST = 1 – 0.2/0.4 = 1 – 0.5 = 0.5

Interpretation: FST = 0.5 falls in the 0.5–1.0 range — indicating stage of subspecies level differentiation. Genetic similarity is very low; populations have largely different allele compositions.
Q6
What does FST < QST tell you about selection on a quantitative trait? Give a real-world context.
FST < QST means quantitative traits are more differentiated between populations than neutral molecular markers. This indicates divergent / disruptive selection — populations have locally adapted their phenotypes beyond what random drift alone would produce. Real-world example: tree populations adapting bud-set timing or cold hardiness to local climates. The phenotypic divergence exceeds neutral expectations → local adaptation (adaptive divergence).
Q7
Explain the Jukes-Cantor dN/dS ratio. What does each scenario (dN/dS < 1, = 1, > 1) mean?
dN = rate of non-synonymous substitutions (amino acid changes); dS = rate of synonymous substitutions (silent changes).

dN/dS = 1: Equal rates → gene is neutral, not under selection
dN/dS > 1: More amino acid changes retained than expected → protein changes are advantageous → positive selection
dN/dS < 1: Fewer amino acid changes than synonymous → most protein changes are harmful and removed → purifying / negative selection

Most genes have dN/dS < 1 because most mutations are deleterious.
Q8
Describe frequency-dependent selection and explain why it leads to oscillating allele frequencies rather than fixation of one allele.
In frequency-dependent selection, the fitness of an allele depends on how common it is. In host-pathogen systems: a rare resistance allele provides protection (pathogens haven't evolved to overcome it) → it increases in frequency → pathogens evolve to infect it → the allele's advantage disappears → pathogens tracking the now-common allele become common → the original allele becomes advantageous again → cycle repeats.

This creates a negative feedback loop: whichever allele is most common becomes the pathogen target, giving the rarer allele an advantage. This prevents either allele from ever fully fixing — both oscillate indefinitely.
Q9
What is the Red Queen hypothesis, and how does it relate to frequency-dependent selection and the maintenance of sexual reproduction?
The Red Queen hypothesis (named from Lewis Carroll) states that hosts and pathogens coevolve in an arms race — each must continuously evolve just to maintain their current fitness against an ever-adapting opponent ("it takes all the running you can do, to keep in the same place").

Connection to frequency-dependent selection: pathogens track the most common host genotype → rare genotypes are favored → allele frequencies oscillate → genetic diversity is maintained.

Connection to sexual reproduction: sexual reproduction creates new genotype combinations each generation, making it harder for pathogens to find a "master key" that infects all hosts. This explains why sexual reproduction has evolved and persisted despite its costs.
Q10
Why does genetic diversity persist in populations despite selection? Name at least four reasons.
Genetic diversity persists because: (1) The favorable allele may still be increasing but hasn't fixed yet — selection takes time; (2) Genetic drift and migration counteract selection, especially in small populations; (3) Fitness depends on many loci simultaneously — no single allele determines everything; (4) The environment is not constant — what's favorable today may not be tomorrow; (5) Multiple forms of selection act simultaneously (e.g., stabilizing in one trait, directional in another); (6) Frequency-dependent selection (Red Queen dynamics) keeps alleles cycling without fixation; (7) Balancing selection actively maintains multiple alleles.
