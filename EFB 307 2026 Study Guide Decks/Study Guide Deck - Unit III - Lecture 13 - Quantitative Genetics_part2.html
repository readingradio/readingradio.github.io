Q1. Write the full equation partitioning VP into its components. Which part does broad-sense heritability use? Which part does narrow-sense heritability use?

VP = VA + VD + VI + VE

H² (broad-sense) = VG / VP = (VA + VD + VI) / VP — uses all genetic variance.
h² (narrow-sense) = VA / VP — uses only additive genetic variance.

Q2. Researchers measure a disease trait. VG = 45, VE = 25. What is VP and H²?

VP = VG + VE = 45 + 25 = 70
H² = VG / VP = 45 / 70 ≈ 0.64

This means ~64% of phenotypic variation in disease incidence is due to genetic differences.

Q3. What percent of alleles are shared between: identical twins, fraternal twins, full siblings, half siblings, and parent-offspring?

Identical (MZ) twins: 100%
Fraternal (DZ) twins: 50%
Full siblings: 50%
Half siblings: 25%
Parent–offspring: 50%
Open-pollinated (OP) half-sibs in trees: ~25–33% depending on mating system.

Q4. Explain the three experimental scenarios in which H² can be calculated. What is being controlled for in each?

1. MZ vs. DZ twin comparison (Falconer's formula): Controls for σ²E by comparing twins raised in the same environment. H² = 2(rMZ − rDZ).

2. Clone trials (ANOVA): Controls for σ²G — all ramets of a clone share 100% of their genetics, so within-clone variation is purely environmental.

3. Highly inbred lines (triple test crosses): Allows direct estimation of σ²D and σ²I, isolating each genetic component.

Q5. In a twin study of singing ability, rMZ = 0.75 for males and rDZ = 0.50. Calculate H² using Falconer's formula. Is H² higher for males or females (rMZ = 0.78, rDZ = 0.60)?

Males: H² = 2(0.75 − 0.50) = 2 × 0.25 = 0.50
Females: H² = 2(0.78 − 0.60) = 2 × 0.18 = 0.36

H² is higher for males. This means a greater proportion of variation in singing ability among males is attributable to genetic differences.

Q6. Using the ANOVA table below (clone trial, 10 ramets per clone), calculate σ²Clone, σ²Error, VP, and H².

Total: SS=7.37, DF=39 | Clones: SS=4.93, DF=7 | Error: SS=2.44, DF=32

MSClones = 4.93/7 = 0.704  |  MSError = 2.44/32 = 0.076

σ²Clone = (MSClones − MSError) / n = (0.704 − 0.076) / 5 = 0.126
σ²Error = MSError = 0.076
VP = 0.126 + 0.076 = 0.202
H² = 0.126 / 0.202 = ≈ 0.62

Note: The published example uses 5 ramets per clone. The calculation approach is the same.

Q7. Write the breeder's equation. A Nicotiana population has mean flower length 51.55 mm. You select plants averaging 45 mm and grow out the offspring, which average 50 mm. What is h²?

R = h² × S → h² = R / S

S = 45 − 51.55 = −6.55 mm (selection differential)
R = 50 − 51.55 = −1.55 mm (observed response)
h² = −1.55 / −6.55 = 0.24

Q8. In midparent regression, how is h² estimated? What does the slope represent?

In midparent-offspring regression, h² = the slope of the regression line (b in y = a + bx), where x = midparent value (average of both parents) and y = offspring mean.

The slope represents the unit change in offspring phenotype per unit change in midparent value. A slope of 1.0 means traits are perfectly inherited; a slope of 0 means offspring phenotype is unrelated to parents (no additive heritability).

Q9. Why is the multiplier different for full-sib vs. half-sib regression when estimating h²? What is the formula for each?

Because h² measures the additive genetic contribution, which must be scaled by how much of the genome individuals share.

Full siblings share 50% of alleles → h² = 2 × slope
Half siblings share 25% of alleles → h² = 4 × slope (or 4ρ in ANOVA)
Open-pollinated (OP) share ~33% → h² = 3 × slope

Dividing by the proportion of alleles shared "scales up" the observed correlation to estimate the full additive genetic variance.

Q10. Using the foliage blight half-sib ANOVA (MSFam = 25, MSError = 2.7, n = 26), calculate h² assuming true half-siblings.

σ²Family = (25 − 2.7) / 26 = 22.3 / 26 = 0.858
σ²Error = 2.7
VP = 0.858 + 2.7 = 3.558
ρ = 0.858 / 3.558 = 0.241
h² = 4ρ = 4 × 0.241 = 0.97

This is suspiciously high! Possible reasons: (1) Biological: foliage blight resistance may be under strong genetic control; (2) Methodological: OP families may not be true half-sibs (some may be full-sibs), inflating the family variance component and thus h².

Q11. A dairy cow population averages 5 gallons/day. h² = 0.25. A selected cow produces 9 gal/day. What is the EBV and expected milk production of her offspring?

S = 9 − 5 = 4 gallons (selection differential)
EBV = h² × S = 0.25 × 4 = 1 gallon
Expected offspring production = population mean + EBV = 5 + 1 = 6 gallons/day

Q12. Write the QST formula and explain how comparing QST to FST informs us about selection.

QST = σ²GB / (σ²GB + 2σ²GW)
where σ²GB = genetic variance among populations, σ²GW = genetic variance within populations.

QST >> FST → Divergent natural selection / local adaptation (populations differ more in the trait than neutral markers predict)
QST ≈ FST → Neutral drift (differentiation consistent with chance alone)
QST << FST → Stabilizing selection (populations are kept similar despite drift)

Q13. What is a breeding zone / seed zone and why does local adaptation matter for forest management?

A breeding zone is a geographic area within which seed can be sourced and planted while maintaining an acceptable level of local adaptation. It represents the "cutoff" on the adaptive performance curve for a provenance.

Seed zones are generalized, climate-based zones that apply to multiple species simultaneously.

Local adaptation matters because trees planted outside their adapted zone may grow poorly or be maladapted to local climate conditions (e.g., wrong frost timing, drought sensitivity). Under climate change, assisted gene flow decisions depend on understanding QST and adaptive differentiation among provenances.
