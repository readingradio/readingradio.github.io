Structure of the Eukaryotic Gene
How genes are organized — from regulatory regions upstream to coding sequence to the downstream 3′ end.
🧬
Central Dogma — Review

The flow of genetic information: DNA → RNA → Protein. Transcription copies the same nucleotide alphabet; translation converts it to a new alphabet (amino acids).

Key rule: The 5′ end of the mRNA becomes the N-terminus of the polypeptide. Three bases = one codon = one amino acid.
Bacteria
mRNA is translated as it is being transcribed — transcription and translation occur simultaneously.
Eukaryotes
Transcription and translation occur at different times and locations (nucleus vs. cytoplasm).

⬆
Upstream of the TSS — The Promoter

Cis-regulatory modules (CRMs)

  • 100s–10,000s bp upstream of TSS
  • Binding sites (enhancers) for sequence-specific transcription factors
  • Control where and when the gene is transcribed
  • Highly variable for different genes
  • Sequences 6–12 bp; some palindromes

Core promoter

  • 25–30 bp upstream of TSS
  • Contains the TATA box (~position −25)
  • Determines if the gene can be transcribed
  • Generally similar across different genes
  • ~25% of human genes have a TATA sequence; others use alternative initiator sequences
Key contrast: CRMs are gene-specific (variable); the core promoter is broadly shared (similar). Mutations in CRMs change the pattern of expression; mutations in the core promoter can silence the gene entirely.
🔤
TF Binding — Consensus Sequences

Each transcription factor has a specific DNA-binding domain that recognizes a consensus sequence. These sequences are:

  • 6–12 bp long
  • Some positions are invariant; others can vary
  • Some are palindromes (recognized by dimeric TFs)
  • Similar but not identical across related species
Antp (Drosophila, helix-turn-helix): TTAATGA
MEFA (Human, MADS α-helix): CTATTATAG
AP1 Fos/Jun (Leucine zipper): TGASTCA

Each TF also has an interacting domain that contacts the Mediator complex — this domain is more similar across TFs.

🪲
Hox Genes — CRMs in Action

Hox genes encode transcription factors that specify body regions. They are co-linear: the order of Hox genes along the chromosome mirrors the body regions they control.

  • Drosophila: 1 cluster (HOM-C)
  • Humans: 4 clusters (A–D), 13 genes each
  • Mutations in CRMs change where a Hox gene is expressed → dramatic homeotic transformations
Antennapedia example: A CRM mutation causes the Antp gene to be expressed in the head → forelegs grow where antennae should be. The coding region is unchanged; only the regulatory region mutated.

This illustrates the broader principle: regulatory (non-coding) mutations can be major drivers of morphological evolution.

Cell Type Differences

All cells in an organism carry the same genome. Differences in cell type (neurons, muscle, red blood cells…) arise because different genes are transcribed. The CRMs are what determine this specificity — different cell types express different transcription factors that bind different CRMs.

Same genome Different TFs per cell type Different genes transcribed Different proteins → different function

Transcription
From chromatin remodeling and TF binding to RNA synthesis, splicing, and post-transcriptional regulation.
🔓
Pre-initiation: Open Chromatin

Before transcription can begin, the chromatin must be accessible. Heterochromatin (tightly packed) is refractory to TF binding; euchromatin (open) is available.

Histone modifications can "lock up" or open chromatin by affecting:

  • Histone–histone interactions
  • Histone–DNA interactions

Common modifications:

  • Acetyl group (–COCH₃)
  • Phosphate group
  • Methyl group (–CH₃)
Epigenetic inheritance: Histone modifications can be heritable — passed to daughter cells without changing the DNA sequence. "Pioneer" TFs can open closed chromatin.
🧩
Pre-initiation Complex Assembly
1

Regulatory TFs bind to their recognition sequences in the CRMs — determines whether and where the gene is activated.

2

TBP (TATA-binding protein, part of TFIID) binds the TATA box of the core promoter — this is the "general" TF common to all protein-coding genes.

3

Mediator complex bridges the distant regulatory TFs and RNA Pol II at the promoter. Chromatin loops bring these regions together.

4

RNA Polymerase II holoenzyme is recruited — the full preinitiation complex forms and transcription begins.

Key logic: Regulatory TFs are gene-specific (variable). TBP + RNA Pol II are used by all protein-coding genes. The Mediator physically connects them.

Post-transcription: RNA Processing

The primary transcript (pre-mRNA) must be processed before export and translation:

5′ Cap
7-methylguanosine cap added co-transcriptionally. Required for export and translation.
Poly-A Tail
String of A's added to 3′ end. Affects transcript stability and translation efficiency.
Splicing
Introns removed, exons joined. Mediated by snRNPs (snRNAs + proteins).
Intron = intervening sequence. Introns are degraded; exons are ligated.
🔀
Alternative Splicing

The same primary transcript can be spliced in multiple ways, generating different protein isoforms from a single gene:

TypeWhat happens
Exon skippingAn exon is included in some transcripts but not others
Alternative 5′ siteDifferent 5′ splice site; extends or shortens exon 1
Alternative 3′ siteDifferent 3′ splice site; extends or shortens exon 2
Mutually exclusive exonsEither exon 2 or exon 3 included — never both
Context: Alternatively spliced isoforms are often present in different cell types or at different developmental stages — a major source of protein diversity without extra genes.
🔇
Non-coding RNAs

snRNAs

  • Small nuclear RNAs
  • Part of the spliceosome
  • Base-pair with pre-mRNA to direct splice site choice

Long ncRNAs

  • Diverse functions
  • Can affect transcriptional initiation
  • Many functions still unknown

microRNAs (miRNAs)

  • ~22 nt small RNAs
  • Base-pair with target mRNA (usually 3′ UTR in animals)
  • Block translation or target mRNA for degradation
  • One mRNA can have multiple miRNA target sites
  • Target site locations are variable in plants
Section 3 of 4
Translation
The ribosome decodes mRNA into protein — initiation, elongation, termination — plus translational and post-translational regulation.
🔤
The Three RNA Players
mRNA
The transcript. Carries the codon sequence. Threaded between ribosomal subunits 5′→3′.
tRNA (~90 nt)
Carries amino acids. Aminoacyl-tRNA synthetases attach the correct AA to the 3′ end based on the anticodon. Anticodon is exposed in the 3-D cloverleaf structure.
rRNA
Site of translation. Forms the ribosome structure with ribosomal proteins. Catalyzes peptide bond formation (ribozyme activity).
Crick's prediction: Before tRNA was discovered, Crick proposed an "adaptor" molecule with an RNA sequence and an amino acid portion — confirmed to be tRNA.
🧪
The Ribosome

Composed of protein and rRNA. Three functional sites:

A site
Aminoacyl — incoming charged tRNA enters here. One codon exposed at a time.
P site
Peptidyl — tRNA carrying the growing polypeptide chain. Start codon (AUG) enters here first.
E site
Exit — uncharged (empty) tRNA leaves the ribosome from here.
Prokaryote ribosomes: 30S + 50S = 70S. Eukaryote: 40S + 60S = 80S. This size difference is exploited by many antibiotics.
▶
Initiation, Elongation, Termination
1

Initiation: The AUG start codon is brought to the P site (not A site) by initiation factor proteins. Met-tRNA occupies P site; second codon is read at A site.

2

Elongation cycle: (a) New charged tRNA enters A site. (b) Peptide bond forms — growing chain transferred to A-site tRNA. (c) Ribosome translocates one codon (E → P → A). Uncharged tRNA exits E site.

3

Termination: Stop codon (UAA, UAG, UGA) enters A site — no tRNA recognizes it. Release factor proteins bind and hydrolyze the peptide chain, releasing the polypeptide.

Direction: Amino acids are added to the C-terminus stepwise. Codons and anticodons are antiparallel.
⚖️
Translational Regulation — Ferritin & Transferrin

Translation can be regulated by proteins that bind to the 5′ or 3′ UTR. Classic iron-sensing example:

Ferritin (iron storage)

  • Low iron: IRP binds to IRE in 5′ UTR → blocks ribosome access → no ferritin made
  • High iron: IRP + Fe cannot bind IRE → ribosome translates ferritin → sequesters free iron

Transferrin (iron import)

  • Low iron: IRP binds IRE in 3′ UTR → stabilizes mRNA → transferrin made → imports iron
  • High iron: IRP + Fe cannot bind → mRNA degraded → no transferrin → no iron import
Elegant logic: The same IRP protein, the same binding motif (IRE), but opposite effects depending on whether the IRE is in the 5′ or 3′ UTR — a single sensor coordinating two opposing responses.
🪰
Translational Regulation — Drosophila Body Axis

hb (hunchback) mRNA is deposited uniformly by the mother. Nanos (Nos) protein forms a posterior-to-anterior gradient and blocks hb translation via the NRE in its 3′ UTR.

  • Anterior: low Nos → hb translated → Hunchback protein made
  • Posterior: high Nos → hb not translated → no Hunchback
Result: Hb protein forms an anterior-to-posterior gradient even though hb mRNA is uniformly distributed. This gradient specifies anterior structures. Homeotic genes then translate these gradients into specific body parts.

Prokaryotes & Recombinant DNA Technology
Operons, reporter genes, restriction enzymes, cloning vectors, and libraries.
📊
Gene Regulation: Bacteria vs. Eukaryotes

Bacteria

  • Regulation primarily at initiation of transcription
  • No nuclear envelope — transcription & translation simultaneous
  • No introns; no splicing

Eukaryotes

  • Regulation at pre-initiation of transcription
  • Plus: RNA splicing, microRNAs, other ncRNAs, translational controls
  • Many more regulatory layers
🧫
Operons — Prokaryotic Gene Clusters

In bacteria, functionally related genes are often co-localized and co-transcribed as a single long mRNA — this is an operon.

trp operon example: Genes trpE–trpD–trpC–trpB–trpA encode enzymes for tryptophan biosynthesis. One upstream promoter drives transcription of all five; each has its own start and stop codon on the single polycistronic mRNA, producing five separate proteins.
One promoter Single long transcript Multiple stop codons Multiple separate proteins
💡
Reporter Genes

A reporter gene is placed under control of a gene's regulatory region to reveal when and where that gene is expressed.

Transcriptional reporter
Regulatory region + reporter coding sequence only. Answers: where/when is the gene transcribed?
Translational reporter
Full gene + reporter fused in-frame. Answers: where/when is the protein found?

Common reporters: GFP (fluorescence), lacZ (β-galactosidase, produces blue color), antibiotic resistance genes.

eat-4 / GFP example: All neurons have the eat-4 gene, but GFP reporter shows it is only transcribed in ~38 specific neurons in C. elegans — governed by 3 distinct CRMs, each controlling expression in different neuronal subsets.
✂️
Tool 1: Restriction Enzymes

Bacterial defense against viruses — over 200 kinds known. Cut double-stranded DNA at specific recognition sequences (usually palindromes).

Cohesive (sticky) ends — staggered cuts (HindIII, BamHI): leave single-stranded overhangs that can anneal with complementary fragments. Enable joining of DNA from different sources.

Blunt ends — cuts both strands at same position (AluI): no overhang; can still be ligated but with lower efficiency.

HindIII cuts: 5'–A↓AGCTT–3' → 5'–A AGCTT–3' 3'–TTCGA↑A–5' → 3'–TTCGA A–5'
Ligation: Complementary sticky ends anneal by base pairing. DNA ligase seals the gaps, forming covalent phosphodiester bonds. This joins DNA from any two sources cut with the same enzyme.
⭕
Tool 2: Cloning Vectors (Plasmids)

Plasmid vectors must have three features:

1

Multiple cloning site (MCS) — a cluster of unique restriction enzyme sites where foreign DNA can be inserted.

2

Origin of replication (ori) — allows the plasmid to replicate independently using host cell enzymes. Plasmid copies are distributed to daughter cells at division.

3

Selectable marker — typically an antibiotic resistance gene (e.g., ampR) to identify cells that took up the plasmid, plus lacZ for blue-white screening: cells with insert disrupt lacZ → white colonies; cells with intact plasmid → blue colonies.

Phage vectors (e.g., λ phage) can carry inserts up to 45 kb — much larger than typical plasmids.

Real-World Application: Impossible Burger™

A concrete example of recombinant DNA technology in food production:

1

Legumes produce leghemoglobin in root nodules (anaerobic environment) to bind O₂ during nitrogen fixation — structurally similar to animal hemoglobin.

2

The soy leghemoglobin gene is isolated and inserted into a plasmid vector.

3

The recombinant plasmid is introduced into Pichia pastoris (yeast), which is grown in bioreactors.

4

Yeast secretes leghemoglobin → purified → added to plant-based burgers to impart meaty flavor, texture, and bioavailable iron.

  • Upstream (5′ / promoter region): Contains cis-regulatory modules (CRMs) and the core promoter. CRMs control where and when the gene is transcribed; the core promoter (TATA box) determines if it can be transcribed.
  • Coding region: From the TSS through the stop codon. Includes 5′ UTR, AUG start, exons & introns, stop codon, and 3′ UTR. Encodes the protein product; UTRs control translation and mRNA stability.
  • Downstream (3′): The downstream region beyond the stop codon. Includes part of the 3′ UTR and beyond; involved in transcription termination signals.

CRMs are binding sites for sequence-specific (regulatory) transcription factors, located 100s–10,000s bp upstream of the TSS. They determine where and when a gene is expressed and are highly variable across different genes — each gene has a unique combination of binding sites.

Core promoter is 25–30 bp upstream of the TSS, contains the TATA box, and is recognized by TBP (part of TFIID). It determines if the gene can be transcribed and is generally similar across different genes — the same TBP and RNA Pol II are used for all protein-coding genes.

In Antennapedia mutants, legs grow where antennae should be. This is caused by a mutation in the CRM (cis-regulatory module) of the Antp gene — specifically, a CRM that normally restricts Antp expression to the thorax becomes expressed in the head.

The protein-coding sequence of Antp is unchanged — the protein still has the same function. The mutation changed where the gene is transcribed (the pattern of expression), not what the protein does. Target genes in head cells are now transcribed as they would be in a foreleg cell → legs form in the head.

  1. Chromatin must be open (euchromatic) — heterochromatin is refractory to TF binding. Pioneer TFs or histone-modifying enzymes remodel chromatin.
  2. Regulatory TFs bind their recognition sequences in the CRMs.
  3. TBP (part of TFIID) binds the TATA box of the core promoter.
  4. The Mediator complex physically bridges the distant regulatory TFs and the core promoter complex. Chromatin loops to bring these regions into proximity.
  5. RNA Polymerase II is recruited; the preinitiation complex forms.
  6. RNA Pol II begins transcription at the TSS.
  • 5′ cap (7-methylguanosine): Added co-transcriptionally. Required for nuclear export, ribosome binding, and protection of the 5′ end from degradation.
  • Poly-A tail: String of adenosines added to the 3′ end. Affects mRNA stability (longer tail = more stable) and translation efficiency.
  • Splicing: Introns are removed and exons are joined by the spliceosome (snRNAs + proteins). Generates a shorter, mature mRNA. Alternative splicing can generate multiple protein isoforms from one gene.
  1. Entry: A new aminoacyl-tRNA with an anticodon complementary to the A-site codon enters the A site.
  2. Peptide bond formation: The ribosome (rRNA, ribozyme activity) catalyzes formation of a peptide bond between the growing chain (on P-site tRNA) and the new amino acid (on A-site tRNA). The growing chain is transferred to the A-site tRNA.
  3. Translocation: The ribosome shifts one codon (3 nt) toward the 3′ end of the mRNA. The tRNA carrying the growing chain moves from A→P; the empty (uncharged) tRNA moves from P→E and exits. A new codon is now at the A site.
  4. Cycle repeats until a stop codon enters the A site.

Both ferritin and transferrin mRNAs contain Iron Response Elements (IREs) that are bound by IRP (Iron Regulatory Protein). The location of the IRE determines the outcome:

  • Ferritin (5′ UTR): Low iron → IRP binds IRE in 5′ UTR → blocks ribosome access → no ferritin (no need to store iron). High iron → IRP-Fe can't bind → ferritin translated → sequesters excess iron.
  • Transferrin (3′ UTR): Low iron → IRP binds IREs in 3′ UTR → stabilizes mRNA → transferrin made → imports more iron. High iron → IRP-Fe can't bind → mRNA degraded → no transferrin → no iron import.

The same IRP sensor, the same IRE motif, but opposite functional outcomes — determined by whether the binding site is in the 5′ or 3′ UTR.

An operon is a cluster of functionally related genes in bacteria (and archaea) that share a single promoter and are transcribed together as one polycistronic mRNA. Each coding region on the mRNA has its own start and stop codon and produces a separate protein.

Differences from eukaryotes:

  • Eukaryotes have one promoter per gene (monocistronic mRNAs)
  • Eukaryotic genes contain introns; bacterial genes do not
  • Eukaryotes have elaborate pre-initiation with Mediator, CRMs, chromatin; bacterial regulation is simpler (sigma factors, repressors/activators)
  • No nuclear compartmentalization in bacteria — translation occurs simultaneously with transcription
  1. Multiple cloning site (MCS): A cluster of unique restriction enzyme recognition sequences. Allows the same enzyme to cut both the vector and the insert, generating complementary sticky ends that can be ligated together. Without this, you can't insert foreign DNA at a defined location.
  2. Origin of replication (ori): Allows the plasmid to replicate using the host cell's replication enzymes, independently of the host chromosome. This ensures the inserted DNA is copied and passed to daughter cells without needing to integrate into the genome.
  3. Selectable marker: Typically an antibiotic resistance gene (e.g., ampicillin resistance). Allows you to select only bacteria that successfully took up a plasmid (colonies on ampicillin plates) vs. those that didn't. LacZ provides blue-white screening to distinguish vectors with vs. without insert.

Genomic library: Made from total genomic DNA cut with restriction enzymes and cloned into vectors. Contains everything — all genes, introns, regulatory regions, repetitive sequences. Use when studying gene structure, regulatory sequences, or when you want to capture all sequences including non-coding regions.

cDNA library: Made from mRNA (reverse-transcribed to cDNA). Represents only expressed genes at a given time and in a given tissue. Contains no introns. Use when studying gene expression profiles, when you want to express a eukaryotic gene in bacteria (bacteria can't splice introns), or for identifying genes active in specific cell types.

Metagenomic library: From environmental DNA of mixed organisms — used to study unculturable microbes or community diversity.
